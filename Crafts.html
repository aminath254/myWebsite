Maldivian crafts



        Maldives has a long history and heritage of different type crafts. They are known to be talented for their unique creations and traditions. These traditions has been passed from one generations to another. Most of the crafts are handmade, without the help of machines and are created using raw materials that are easily available.








Boat construction

Boats, also known as Dhoni in the local language are wooden handbuilt sea vessels. They are said to be inpired by Arabian sailing dhow which was a popular trading vessel in the olden days. They are made by coconut palm timbres and have multiple purposes such as fishing.

how its made

Boats (dhonis ) are mostly contructed without the help of designs or plans which is quite facinating. It is made by highly skilled crafters in carpentaries by depending on the size, type and pliability of the wood for directions. The head of the carpentry would give intructions and measurements to the workers, this usually takes up to 60 days to finish. Boats are traditionally made from timbre, which was the only boat building material that was readily available. Sometimes the size of boat depends on the height and the avaibility of coconut timbres. back then. However, the timbre found in the Maldives is not the most ideal for boat building.


Uses

Boats are known to be the oldest sea vessel and they have several purposes. The triangular shaped latin ring sails on the boat makes it alot easier for the boat to move forward because it catches the wind. They are mainly used for fishing but In the early days they were the main source used for transportion. People go from one island to another and instead of GPS and compasses the captains mostly relied on the labyrinths of corals and by looking the sky which helps them to understand the directions of north, east, south and west. This may take days, weeks and even months to reach the final destination depending on the distance.










Mat weaving

Mat meaving, also known as ThunduKunaa has been one of the most acient and unique art made by Maldivian woman. It mostly comes with yellow, black, brown and orange color, and is made using raw materials that is easily available.









How weaves are made

The mat is made from a local reed also known as haa or khau in the local language. Mat weavers first pluck the reeds and wash them. Then that they are left in the sun to dry them which usually takes about four days. After that the reeds are dyed innvarious colors using from fermented coconut water, types of leaves and locally found roots. The Fiori located in the Gaafu Dhaalu Atoll are said to have the best reeds for making thundu kunaa.



Uses

Mat weaving (thundu kunaa) is traditionally used for sleeping, seating or praying. Now they are usually gifted for visiting foreign dignitaries.




Laquer work

Lacquer work, also known as liyelaa jehun or laa jehun in the local language, is a beautiful craft done by maldivians. This craft is said to be inherited from the Chinese culture.



It consists of 2 stages: 1.



1. Preparation of laa

First laqeur is melted and the pigment is added. After it cools down, it is mixed with the pigment. Then it is flattened and cut into stripes to add colors.


2. Liyuna and laa jehun

There is no specific kind of wood for this. The wood for sculpting is first attached to the end with meted laquer, the rope is pulled for spinning. Each tools have a tip in different shapes and sizes for different purposes and they have to be used with precision for a smooth final product. After the wood is sculpted, laquer is applied as smooth as possible. The first layer is usually in yellow, any color can be applied to the second layer. Then the layers are polished. At last the designs are added.
